The Pattern

The problem we're solving

Apollo is a private equity firm. Before they invest hundreds of millions of dollars in a company, a team of analysts spends 2-3 weeks doing intensive due diligence — reading hundreds of documents, answering critical questions, and coordinating with dozens of external advisors. Today, all of this is managed in a single Excel spreadsheet emailed between team members. The spreadsheet has no connection to the documents that contain the answers.

14

Critical questions

Seven gating items that can kill a deal, plus seven additional questions that shape the investment thesis. Each needs a definitive, evidence-backed answer.

10+

External workstreams

Consultants (Bain), financial auditors (PwC), M&A advisors, banks, operating partners — each producing reports and findings on different timelines.

2-3

Weeks to decide

From the moment a deal enters diligence, the team has 2-3 weeks to reach a go or no-go investment decision. Every day of admin overhead is a day lost from analysis.

The associate's daily reality

The junior analyst (called an "associate") is the person who does most of the work. Their job is to be the glue between three things: the questions the deal team needs answered, the external advisors doing the research, and the documents that contain the actual evidence.

Today, this means: open a document storage system called Box, find the latest report from PwC, read it, figure out which of the 14 questions it helps answer, switch to Excel, type in the finding, update the status, save the file with today's date, and email it to the team. Then repeat — for every document, every day, across every active deal.

The product we're building eliminates this manual loop. AI reads the documents, matches findings to questions, and shows everyone the same live dashboard. The associate's time shifts from admin to analysis.

Two layers of the system

The framework has two layers. The top layer describes what happens to the deal as it moves through diligence — the "deal journey." The bottom layer describes what the software does at each stage — the "system capabilities." They map to each other, but they're different lenses: one is about the business process, the other is about the technology.

The System's Job — three AI capabilities

Covers Stages 1 + 2

Define

When a deal is created, the system auto-generates a set of due diligence questions based on the deal type. About 90% of questions are standard across all deals; 10% are customised. It also sets up workstream categories and a dedicated email address for document uploads.

Powers Stage 3

Retrieve

Every night, the system processes new documents that have been uploaded to the deal's storage folder. It reads each document, extracts relevant data points, and matches them against the outstanding questions — surfacing findings with confidence scores and links back to the original source.

Powers Stage 4

Orchestrate

The system tracks which questions have been answered, which are still open, and which might need revisiting. It generates end-of-day and end-of-week summaries, flags items that have gone stale, and shows what changed since the last time each user logged in.

Stage 5

No AI equivalent

The decision to invest is a human judgment call. The system's job is to get the work package to a state where that decision can be made confidently — not to make it. Apollo's requirements explicitly state: "the system will not autonomously complete due diligence."

Why two layers matter: The deal journey (Trigger → Decide) tells you why each capability exists and who benefits from it. The system capabilities (Define, Retrieve, Orchestrate) tell you what needs to be built and how the technology works. When discussing the product with Apollo's business stakeholders, use the deal journey. When briefing the engineering team or the designer, use the system capabilities.

How a question gets answered

Each of the 14 due diligence questions moves through a lifecycle of states. This is not a simple open/closed model — because in the real world, new evidence can arrive that contradicts an answer the team already confirmed. The system needs to handle this gracefully without overwriting human decisions.

Unanswered

The question exists but nobody has provided an answer yet. It was auto-generated when the deal was created. This is the starting state for every question.

AI-Suggested

The system found something in a document that might answer this question. It shows the relevant extract with a confidence score and a link to the original source. It's waiting for a human to review it.

Confirmed

An associate reviewed the AI suggestion and marked it as the definitive answer — the "golden answer." This is now the authoritative response. Only a human can set this state.

Challenged

A new document arrived that contains information conflicting with the confirmed answer. The original answer is preserved. The new finding is flagged for review. The associate decides whether to update or dismiss.

How this plays out in practice

Typical scenario

A new question gets answered

1
A deal is created and 14 questions are automatically generated. All start as Unanswered.
2
Overnight, the system processes 3 new documents uploaded to the deal's Box folder by external advisors.
3
The AI matches relevant findings to 5 of the 14 questions. Those 5 move to AI-Suggested.
4
Next morning, the associate logs in and sees "5 new suggestions" in their morning briefing.
5
They review each one: confirm 4 as golden answers, reject 1 (it goes back to Unanswered).
Conflict scenario

New evidence contradicts a confirmed answer

1
Last week, the associate confirmed the "Cost Structure" answer based on data from a management presentation.
2
Overnight, PwC uploads a new financial audit report to the deal's Box folder.
3
The AI finds cost data in the PwC report that contradicts what the management team presented.
4
The question moves to Challenged. The original confirmed answer is preserved — nothing is overwritten.
5
The associate reviews both sources, then either updates the golden answer or dismisses the new finding.
Supporting evidence

New data strengthens an existing answer

1
The "Competition" question was confirmed last week based on Bain's market study.
2
A new industry report is uploaded to Box with additional competitive data.
3
The AI recognises the new data is consistent with the confirmed answer — no contradiction.
4
The new source is added to the evidence chain. The answer stays Confirmed.
5
The associate sees "1 new supporting source" — helpful context, no action required.
Coverage gap

A question stays unanswered too long

1
"Tech Debt" has been Unanswered for 5 days. No documents in Box contain relevant evidence.
2
The system flags it as stale — it appears prominently in the morning briefing.
3
The dashboard shows which workstream should be providing this information (e.g., "Technology advisor — no deliverable received").
4
The associate can see who needs to be chased and can escalate directly from the dashboard.
5
Once the advisor submits their report, the system processes it in the next overnight batch.
The key design principle: The system must never silently overwrite a human-confirmed answer. But it must surface when new evidence contradicts one. The UI should frame this as "new data is available for your review" — not "your answer was wrong." The associate stays in control at every step.

From spreadsheet to product

Three layers of translation: what Apollo uses today, what we're building, and how our framework ties it together.

What exists today — Apollo's Excel tracker

Tab 1: Overview

The deal's objective, 7 gating items, and 7 additional questions. This is the "what do we need to know?" layer — the requirements definition for the entire diligence process.

Tab 2: Workstream Tracker

Operational tasks split by advisor or team. Bain, PwC, banks, M&A advisors — each with status, timing, and notes. This is the "who's doing what?" layer.

Tab 3: Granular Workplan

Each gating item broken into specific sub-questions with assigned owners. This is a drill-down of Tab 1 — more detail on the same questions, not a separate concept.

Module 1: Project Tracker

A high-level view of all active deals and where each one sits in its lifecycle. Think of it like Jira for investments — roughly 10 stages from deal creation to completion.

Module 2: Diligence Tracker

The core value driver. AI-surfaced findings matched to due diligence questions. Four answer states (unanswered, suggested, confirmed, challenged). The "golden answer" system.

Links to Apollo's 30 existing AI agents for private equity research. In the MVP, this is just a collection of links — no deep integration. It sits alongside the main workflow as a utility, not as a core feature. Explicitly flagged as a scope creep risk.

How our framework ties it together

Define → Instantiate

Auto-create the work package when a deal is created. Generate the standard question set, build workstream categories, set up the Box email address, and process the associate's initial document upload.

Retrieve → Enrich

Each night, process new Box documents. Extract findings and match them to outstanding questions. Handle the four answer states — including surfacing conflicts with previously confirmed answers without overwriting them.

Orchestrate → Orchestrate

Generate the morning briefing: what changed overnight, which questions moved state, what's been stale too long. Show progress by workstream. Generate end-of-day and end-of-week summaries. Surface when the work package is decision-ready.

The relationship between the three layers: The Excel tabs describe what exists today. The modules describe what we're building. The framework (Define → Retrieve → Orchestrate) describes why each module exists and how they connect. Every design decision should answer the question: does this help the work package move from creation to decision faster?

Boundaries that shape the design

Four real-world constraints that will directly affect every screen and interaction. Each one is sourced from a specific document or conversation with the Apollo team.

🕑

Processing happens overnight, not in real time

Source: Apollo's Designer Brief — Data Freshness Model

When a document is uploaded to Box during the day, it isn't processed immediately. AI analysis runs as a nightly batch job. Every member of the deal team sees the exact same state when they log in the next morning — there are no real-time updates during the workday.

What this means for the design: The primary entry point for associates each morning should be a "what changed overnight" briefing — not a live feed. Think of the experience as opening a newspaper, not watching a stock ticker. New AI suggestions, challenged answers, and stale flags all appear here.
📤

Creating a deal is a multi-step process

Source: Internal design review — flagged by Ilija (lead designer)

When someone taps "New Deal," it's not a simple form. The associate needs to name the deal, upload an initial batch of documents, and then review the AI-generated questions and workstreams before confirming. This is the handoff point where "Define" passes to "Retrieve" — the system's first opportunity to demonstrate its value.

What this means for the design: The deal creation flow should feel like a guided setup wizard, not a blank form. Step 1: name and categorise. Step 2: upload initial documents. Step 3: review AI-generated questions and workstreams. Step 4: confirm and launch. Each step should feel purposeful.
🔗

The Agent Panel is a utility, not a core feature

Source: Apollo's Designer Brief — Module 3 scoping

Apollo has 30 existing AI agents for private equity research tasks. Module 3 of the product provides links to these agents. It does not integrate into the core workflow and has been explicitly flagged as a scope creep risk by Apollo's product lead (Cemre Erbay). In the MVP, it's links only.

What this means for the design: Position the Agent Panel as a sidebar or expandable drawer — accessible but never central. Don't design enrichment or orchestration features around it. If Module 3 were removed entirely, the product should still function perfectly. Design accordingly.
⚠️

We build the front-end only

Source: Business Requirements — Scope Boundary

Conquer builds the user interface and front-end application. Apollo's internal team (led by Jason Mather, Head of AI Engineering) builds and owns the back-end: the AI processing, the Box/Glean integration, and the data pipeline. There will be no DealCloud integration. Box, Outlook, and DealCloud remain Apollo's systems of record — this product does not replace any of them.

What this means for the design: Every screen must assume data comes from an API that Conquer doesn't control. Design for graceful degradation — what does the UI show when the API is slow, when data is incomplete, or when a batch job hasn't run yet? The exact API contract between Conquer's front-end and Apollo's back-end is not yet defined.

A pattern we've seen before

Apollo's deal tracker is a specific instance of a problem Conquer has solved across multiple industries. In every case, the core structure is the same: a "work package" starts as raw input, gets enriched by multiple people from multiple sources under time pressure, and ends as a decision-ready output. The tools differ, but the workflow shape is identical.

Lingoto A financial trade A trading signal appears on the order management system Calculations run, risk is assessed, compliance checks are passed, approvals are collected An executed trade
ITAU An investment signal An analyst spots something interesting in their sector Research is gathered, data is analysed, a thesis is built, a pitch deck is created An investment pitch presented to decision-makers

Apollo A PE deal in diligence An opportunity passes screening Documents gathered, questions answered, workstreams tracked, advisors coordinated A go/no-go investment decision
An honest note on this analogy: This pattern is useful for explaining why Conquer has relevant experience — we've built enrichment and orchestration systems before. But the enrichment processes across these clients are fundamentally different: editorial work at News UK, quantitative calculation at Lingoto, analytical research at Apollo. The pattern shows where to look for value. The specific product design comes from Apollo's actual documents, users, and constraints — not from cross-client analogy.
